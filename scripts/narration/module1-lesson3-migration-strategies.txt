Not every migration needs the same level of specification. The amount of spec work varies by migration strategy, and understanding this helps you invest effort where it matters most.

Some strategies need light evidence, not full behavioral reconstruction. Retiring an application requires only the decision rationale and business impact. Retaining requires documentation of the current state and support model. Rehosting needs infrastructure mapping and a dependency list. Replatforming focuses on the adaptation points that will actually change.

Higher-spec strategies depend on understanding behavior before redesign. Refactoring means preserving behavior while redesigning architecture. Re-architecting means decomposing the domain and reframing the target system. Rebuilding requires deep behavioral specification because the implementation is entirely new. And replacing with a SaaS product requires a gap analysis between what the SaaS covers and what still needs custom business capability.

Spec-driven migration adds the most value for refactor, rearchitect, rebuild, and complex replacement work — wherever understanding behavior is the critical path to a safe decision.

The decision process matters just as much: run a lightweight specification pass first to understand complexity. Then choose the strategy based on evidence. Do not start with the target architecture and hope the legacy analysis catches up later. Strategy changes midstream are often a signal that the initial evidence was weak.

The deeper the behavioral change, the deeper the specification requirement. Shallow specs may be enough when infrastructure moves and behavior stays intact. Deep specs are mandatory when you are rebuilding or redesigning behavior. Tooling, governance, and validation all follow from the chosen spec depth.

Match the depth of your specification to the depth of your migration strategy. A rehost does not need a full behavior spec. A rearchitect absolutely does.
